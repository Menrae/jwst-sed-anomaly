\begin{abstract}

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has produced the deepest and most
complete rest-frame optical/near-infrared photometric catalogs of
high-redshift galaxies to date, enabling systematic searches for sources
whose spectral energy distributions (SEDs) deviate from standard
population-synthesis expectations. We present a data-driven pipeline that
identifies statistically anomalous SEDs among $z \gtrsim 0.5$ galaxies in
the CEERS and JADES JWST/NIRCam photometric catalogs. Photometric redshifts
and best-fit template SEDs are derived with EAZY; per-band residuals
between observed and best-fit photometry are then scored for anomalies
using an ensemble of Isolation Forest and UMAP+DBSCAN, and cross-matched
against known active galactic nucleus (AGN) and emission-line-galaxy
catalogs to separate genuine outliers from well-understood astrophysical
contaminants. We report the resulting anomaly rate as a function of
redshift and characterize the residual "unexplained" population that
survives contaminant removal. We interpret this population within a
deliberately conservative framework for galactic-scale technosignature and
biosignature search limits, noting that even a well-characterized null
result constitutes a quantitative upper limit on non-standard astrophysical
processes at cosmological scale. This placeholder abstract will be updated
once the Phase 3/4 analysis on the full real MAST catalogs is complete.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

[PLACEHOLDER --- JWST context] The James Webb Space Telescope has
transformed the study of galaxy evolution at $z > 5$ by providing
sensitive, high-angular-resolution NIRCam and MIRI imaging across a
wavelength baseline unattainable from the ground or with prior space
missions. Large-area imaging surveys such as CEERS \citep{Finkelstein2023},
JADES \citep{Eisenstein2023}, and COSMOS-Web \citep{Casey2023} have
assembled photometric catalogs of hundreds of thousands of galaxies
spanning cosmic noon through the epoch of reionization. These catalogs are
large and homogeneous enough to support systematic, population-level
searches for statistically rare objects, rather than only targeted
follow-up of individually identified curiosities.

[PLACEHOLDER --- ML anomaly detection in astrophysics] Unsupervised machine
learning methods have an established track record of surfacing unusual
sources in large astronomical datasets that would be impractical to find by
visual inspection alone, from outlier detection in SDSS spectra
\citep{BaronPoznanski2017} to searches for spectroscopically unusual
quasars \citep{Meusinger2012}. Applying similar techniques to JWST
photometric residuals --- the difference between observed fluxes and the
best-fit template SED at each source's photometric redshift --- offers a
principled, reproducible way to flag galaxies whose broadband colors are
not well described by standard stellar population synthesis models
\citep{Reyes2023}, without requiring a predefined list of what an "anomaly"
should look like.

[PLACEHOLDER --- astrobiology motivation] A small subset of any such
anomaly sample may, in principle, be astrophysically unexplained even after
accounting for known contaminants such as AGN activity or strong
emission-line contamination of broadband photometry. While the
overwhelming prior expectation is that such residual anomalies reflect
systematics in photometry, photometric redshifts, or template libraries, we
adopt the deliberately conservative position that a well-characterized,
reproducible anomaly pipeline is also the correct tool with which to place
quantitative, galactic-scale upper limits on exotic interpretations,
including technosignatures \citep{Wright2014} and biosignature-adjacent
astrophysical scenarios \citep{LingamLoeb2019, Lacki2016}. This paper does
not claim any detection; its purpose is methodological: to demonstrate a
reproducible anomaly-detection pipeline and to characterize, at current
survey depth, how large an unexplained population remains after standard
astrophysical explanations are exhausted.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{SED Fitting}
\label{sec:sed-fitting}

[PLACEHOLDER] Photometric redshifts and best-fit template SEDs are derived
using EAZY \citep{Brammer2008} with the \texttt{tweak\_fsps\_QSF\_12\_v3}
template set, itself built on Bruzual \& Charlot stellar population
synthesis models \citep{BruzualCharlot2003}. [PLACEHOLDER: describe final
adopted redshift grid, template combination weighting, and any priors
applied.]

\subsection{Residual Extraction}
\label{sec:residual-extraction}

[PLACEHOLDER] For each source, per-band photometric residuals are computed
as $(f_{\rm obs} - f_{\rm model}) / \sigma_{\rm obs}$ relative to a smooth
continuum model fit through the observed photometry, following the general
SED-fitting residual framework reviewed by \citet{Conroy2013}. These
residual vectors form the feature space for the anomaly detectors described
below.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection}
\label{sec:anomaly-detection}

[PLACEHOLDER] We score each source's residual vector using an ensemble of
two complementary unsupervised anomaly detectors: an Isolation Forest,
which isolates anomalous points via random recursive partitioning, and a
UMAP dimensionality reduction followed by DBSCAN density-based clustering,
which flags points that fail to join any density-connected cluster in the
reduced embedding. Combining a global, tree-based method with a local,
density-based method mirrors approaches used elsewhere in astronomical
outlier detection \citep{BaronPoznanski2017, Reyes2023}.

\subsection{Contaminant Removal}
\label{sec:contaminant-removal}

[PLACEHOLDER] Flagged sources are cross-matched on sky position against a
reference AGN catalog (Milliquas) to identify anomalies plausibly explained
by AGN continuum/emission-line contamination, and are separately checked
for redshifted strong emission lines (e.g., Ly$\alpha$) falling within a
broadband filter, which can locally inflate a single band's flux and
masquerade as an SED anomaly \citep{Meusinger2012}. Sources matching either
criterion are retained in the catalog but flagged as "explained" anomalies,
distinct from the residual "unexplained" population discussed in
Section~\ref{sec:results-unexplained}.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

[PLACEHOLDER] We discuss the interpretation of the unexplained anomaly
population in the context of (i) residual systematics in photometry,
photometric redshifts, or template libraries; (ii) known but uncatalogued
astrophysical contaminants (e.g., strong lensing, blended sources, rare
transient contamination); and (iii) the deliberately conservative galactic-
scale technosignature/biosignature interpretive framework motivated in
Section~\ref{sec:intro} \citep{Wright2014, LingamLoeb2019, Lacki2016}. We
emphasize that a well-characterized null result is itself a meaningful,
quantitative constraint on the prevalence of non-standard processes at
current survey depth and sample size.
